Idea 01

Rational and irrational numbers

Every number you'll ever plot on a line — negative, positive, whole, or fractional — is a real number. Real numbers split neatly into two families.

  • Rational numbers can be written as p/q, where p and q are integers and q ≠ 0. Example: 1/2, -3, 0.75.
  • Irrational numbers cannot be written as a simple fraction at all. Example: √2, π.

Both families live on the same number line, side by side — irrational numbers just don't land on a "neat" fraction point.

In simple words

If you can write a number as one whole number sitting over another, it's rational. If you genuinely can't — no matter how hard you try — it's irrational.

Idea 02

Decimal expansions: how to spot a rational number

Divide the numerator by the denominator of any rational number, and only two things can happen to the decimal:

  • It terminates — it just stops. Example: 1/4 = 0.25, 7/8 = 0.875.
  • It repeats a block of digits forever. Example: 1/3 = 0.333…, 1/7 = 0.142857142857….

An irrational number's decimal does neither — it goes on forever without ever settling into a repeating pattern, e.g. π = 3.14159265….

In simple words

Terminates or repeats → rational. Goes on forever with no pattern → irrational. That single test settles it every time.

Idea 03

A quick look at why √2 can't be rational

This is one of the oldest proofs in mathematics, and it uses a neat trick called proof by contradiction — assume the opposite is true, then show it breaks.

  1. Suppose, just for argument's sake, that √2 = p/q, fully reduced (p and q share no common factor).
  2. Squaring both sides: 2 = p²/q², so p² = 2q² — meaning p² is even, so p itself must be even.
  3. If p is even, write p = 2k. Substituting back shows q must be even too.
  4. But that means p and q share a common factor of 2 — contradicting step 1, where we said they had none.

The only way out of that contradiction is that our starting assumption was wrong — so √2 cannot be written as p/q. It's irrational.

In simple words

We assumed √2 was a "neat fraction," followed the logic honestly, and it broke itself. That broken ending is exactly what proves it isn't one.

Idea 04

Euclid's division lemma

For any two positive integers a and b, there exist unique whole numbers q (quotient) and r (remainder) such that:

a = bq + r, where 0 ≤ r < b

This is really just long division, stated formally. Example: dividing 23 by 5 gives 23 = 5(4) + 3 — so q = 4, r = 3.

Applied repeatedly, this lemma becomes Euclid's algorithm — a fast way to find the HCF of two numbers by dividing again and again until the remainder hits 0.

In simple words

It's the "how many times, and what's left over" idea from ordinary division — just written as one clean equation.

Idea 05

The Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic

Every whole number greater than 1 can be broken down into prime numbers in exactly one way (ignoring the order you write them in). That breakdown is its prime factorisation.

Example: 60 = 2² × 3 × 5 — no other combination of primes multiplies to 60.

  • This "one and only one way" fact is why prime factorisation is so reliable for finding HCF and LCM.
  • HCF = multiply the smallest power of each common prime.
  • LCM = multiply the largest power of every prime that appears.
In simple words

Primes are the "atoms" of numbers — every number is built from a unique combination of them, and that uniqueness is what makes the whole theorem useful.

Quick recap

1Rational = p/q, irrational = not p/q 2Terminating or repeating decimal → rational 3√2 is irrational (proof by contradiction) 4Euclid: a = bq + r 5Every number has one unique prime factorisation
