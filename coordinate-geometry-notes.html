Idea 01

The coordinate plane, quickly revisited

Every point on a flat plane can be pinned down with exactly two numbers: (x, y) — how far to move sideways, and how far to move up or down, from the origin (0, 0).

  • The horizontal line is the x-axis; the vertical line is the y-axis.
  • Positive x means right, negative x means left. Positive y means up, negative y means down.

This simple grid is what lets algebra and geometry talk to each other — every shape becomes a set of coordinates, and every equation becomes a picture.

In simple words

Think of (x, y) as directions to a treasure: "x steps this way, y steps that way" — and you land exactly on the point.

Idea 02

The distance formula

To find the straight-line distance between two points, treat the horizontal and vertical gaps as the two legs of a right triangle, then apply Pythagoras' theorem:

d = √[(x₂-x₁)² + (y₂-y₁)²]

Example: distance between (1, 2) and (4, 6) is √[(4-1)² + (6-2)²] = √(9+16) = √25 = 5.

In simple words

It's Pythagoras in disguise — the "sideways gap" and "up-down gap" between two points are just the two shorter sides of a hidden right triangle.

Idea 03

Section formula and its midpoint special case

If a point P divides the segment joining A(x₁,y₁) and B(x₂,y₂) in the ratio m:n, then:

P = ( (mx₂+nx₁)/(m+n) , (my₂+ny₁)/(m+n) )

When P is exactly halfway (ratio 1:1), this collapses into the friendlier midpoint formula:

Midpoint = ( (x₁+x₂)/2 , (y₁+y₂)/2 )

In simple words

The midpoint formula is really just "average the x's, average the y's." The section formula is the same idea, weighted for an uneven split.

Idea 04

Area of a triangle, straight from its coordinates

Given the three vertices A(x₁,y₁), B(x₂,y₂), C(x₃,y₃), the enclosed area is:

Area = ½ |x₁(y₂-y₃) + x₂(y₃-y₁) + x₃(y₁-y₂)|

The vertical bars mean "take the positive value" — area can never come out negative, even if the arithmetic inside does.

Bonus: if this formula ever gives 0, the three points aren't a triangle at all — they're sitting on a single straight line (collinear).

In simple words

No protractor needed — plug in three coordinate pairs, and this one formula hands you the exact area.

Idea 05

Slope, and the equation of a line

The slope (m) of a line tells you how steeply it rises or falls — "rise over run" between any two of its points:

m = (y₂-y₁) / (x₂-x₁)

Once you know the slope and one point (x₁,y₁) the line passes through, its full equation is:

y - y₁ = m(x - x₁)

A positive slope climbs left-to-right, a negative slope falls, and a slope of 0 is perfectly flat.

In simple words

Slope is just "for every step sideways, how many steps up or down" — one number that fully describes a line's tilt.

Quick recap

1A point is just (x, y) on a grid 2Distance: d = √[(x₂-x₁)²+(y₂-y₁)²] 3Midpoint = average of the two x's and y's 4Area from 3 coordinates — 0 means collinear 5Slope m = rise / run
Grids make this friendlier than it looks.

Once distance and slope feel natural, area and the line equation are just small extensions of the same coordinate habit.
