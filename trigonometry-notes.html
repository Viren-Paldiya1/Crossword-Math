Idea 01

sin, cos, tan — ratios of a right triangle

Trigonometry starts with one simple idea: in a right triangle, the ratio between any two sides depends only on one of the other angles, θ — never on the triangle's actual size.

  • sin θ = opposite / hypotenuse
  • cos θ = adjacent / hypotenuse
  • tan θ = opposite / adjacent

A common memory trick: SOH‑CAH‑TOA — Sin=Opp/Hyp, Cos=Adj/Hyp, Tan=Opp/Adj.

In simple words

Pick an angle in a right triangle, and these three ratios are just fixed "recipes" — same angle, same ratio, no matter how big or small the triangle is drawn.

Idea 02

The reciprocal ratios: cosec, sec, cot

Every one of the first three ratios has a flipped-over partner — just 1 divided by that ratio.

  • cosec θ = 1 / sin θ = hypotenuse / opposite
  • sec θ = 1 / cos θ = hypotenuse / adjacent
  • cot θ = 1 / tan θ = adjacent / opposite

You rarely need to memorise these separately — just remember they're reciprocals, and flip the ratio you already know.

In simple words

Six names, but really only three ideas — the second three are just the first three turned upside down.

Idea 04

The identity that ties it all together

Take any right triangle with hypotenuse 1. By Pythagoras' theorem, opposite² + adjacent² = 1². Substitute in the ratios and you get the single most-used identity in trigonometry:

sin²θ + cos²θ = 1

Two useful cousins follow by dividing through by sin²θ or cos²θ:

  • 1 + tan²θ = sec²θ
  • 1 + cot²θ = cosec²θ
In simple words

It's just Pythagoras wearing a trigonometry costume — the triangle's sides squared and added always come back to 1, once the hypotenuse is 1.

Idea 05

Heights and distances — where this earns its keep

The whole point of building these ratios is to measure things you can't reach with a tape measure — a tower's height, a ship's distance from shore, a cliff's depth.

  • Angle of elevation: the angle you tilt your eyes up to see the top of something tall.
  • Angle of depression: the angle you tilt your eyes down to see something below you.

Example: standing 30 m from a tower, if the angle of elevation to its top is 30°, the height is h = 30 × tan 30° = 30 / √3 ≈ 17.3 m — no ladder required.

In simple words

One measured distance, one measured angle, and trigonometry hands you the height or distance you couldn't reach directly.
