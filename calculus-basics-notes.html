Idea 01

What is a limit?

A limit asks a very specific question: as x sneaks closer and closer to some value a — without necessarily ever landing exactly on it — what value does the function get closer and closer to?

limx→a f(x) = L

Example: for f(x) = (x²-1)/(x-1), plugging in x = 1 directly gives 0/0 (undefined) — but simplify first: f(x) = x+1 for every x ≠ 1, so the limit as x → 1 is simply 2.

In simple words

A limit is about the neighbourhood around a point, not the point itself — even if the function trips at exactly x = a, the limit can still exist.

Idea 02

The derivative: slope of the tangent line

The derivative of a function at a point is the slope of the line that just barely touches the curve there — the tangent. It's built directly from the limit idea:

f′(x) = limh→0 [f(x+h) - f(x)] / h

That fraction is just "rise over run" between two nearby points on the curve — the limit is what happens as those two points get infinitely close together, turning a secant line into a tangent line.

Physically, if f(x) is distance travelled, f′(x) is instantaneous speed at that exact moment.

In simple words

A derivative is just "slope, zoomed in until the two points you're comparing basically merge into one."

Idea 03

Rules that make differentiation quick

Instead of the limit definition every time, a handful of shortcut rules cover almost everything you'll differentiate:

  • Power rule: d/dx(xⁿ) = nxⁿ⁻¹
  • Sum/difference rule: d/dx(f ± g) = f′ ± g′
  • Constant rule: the derivative of any plain number is 0 (it doesn't change, so its rate of change is nothing).

Example: for f(x) = 3x³ - 5x + 7, applying these rules term by term gives f′(x) = 9x² - 5.

In simple words

Bring the power down as a multiplier, then drop the power by one — that single move handles most of what you'll ever differentiate.

Idea 04

Where a curve rises, falls, or turns

The sign of the derivative tells you the curve's direction, without needing to look at a graph at all:

  • f′(x) > 0 → the function is increasing (climbing).
  • f′(x) < 0 → the function is decreasing (falling).
  • f′(x) = 0 → a possible turning point — a local maximum or minimum.

To tell max from min at that turning point, check the sign of f′ just before and just after: falling-then-rising means a minimum, rising-then-falling means a maximum. This is exactly how businesses find the price that maximises profit.

In simple words

Setting the derivative to 0 finds the "flat" moments — the very top of a hill or the very bottom of a valley on the curve.

Idea 05

Integration: undoing a derivative

Integration is differentiation run backwards — given a rate of change, it recovers the original function. It uses the same power rule, flipped:

∫ xⁿ dx = xⁿ⁺¹/(n+1) + C

That mysterious + C exists because differentiating any constant gives 0 — so once you reverse the process, you can never be 100% sure which constant was originally there. It represents every possible "parallel" version of the curve.

Example: ∫ 2x dx = x² + C — check it by differentiating x² + C, and you land right back on 2x.

In simple words

If differentiating is "find the slope," integrating is "given the slope everywhere, rebuild the original curve" — with one unavoidable blind spot: the constant.

Idea 06

The definite integral: area under a curve

Add limits to an integral, and it stops being a family of functions and becomes one exact number — the area trapped between the curve and the x-axis, from x = a to x = b:

∫ab f(x) dx = F(b) - F(a)

where F is any antiderivative of f. This shortcut — evaluate the antiderivative at both ends and subtract — is called the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, and it's the bridge that connects "slope" and "area" as two faces of the same idea.

In simple words

Instead of chopping the region under a curve into a million thin rectangles and adding them up, this one theorem gets you the exact area in two quick substitutions.
