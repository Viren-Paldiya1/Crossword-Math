Idea 01

What is a quadratic equation?

It's just an equation where the biggest power of x is x² — nothing higher, like x³, is allowed.

  • Quadratic: x² − 5x + 6 = 0, 2x² + 3 = 0
  • Not quadratic: x + 2 = 0 (highest power is 1), x³ − 1 = 0 (power is 3)

When you draw a quadratic equation on a graph, it always makes a smooth U‑shaped curve, called a parabola — like the yellow curve beside these notes.

In simple words

If you see an "x²" anywhere in the equation, and nothing bigger, it's quadratic. That's really the whole test.

Idea 02

The standard form: ax² + bx + c = 0

Every quadratic equation can be tidied into this one shape. Here, a, b and c are just ordinary numbers.

  • a is the number in front of x², and it can never be 0 (or the x² disappears).
  • b is the number in front of x.
  • c is the number left standing alone.

Example: in 3x² − 4x + 1 = 0, we simply have a = 3, b = −4, c = 1.

In simple words

Think of it like a labelled shelf: once your equation is arranged in this order, picking out a, b and c is just reading three numbers off the shelf.

Idea 03

Finding the roots (solutions)

A "root" is simply a value of x that makes the equation true — on the graph, it's exactly where the curve touches the ground line (the x‑axis).

One easy way to find roots is factorisation. Example: x² − 5x + 6 = 0

  • Split it into two brackets: (x − 2)(x − 3) = 0
  • Either bracket can be zero, so x = 2 or x = 3

Two other tools exist for harder equations — completing the square and the quadratic formula — but factorisation is the friendliest place to start.

In simple words

Roots just answer the question: "at what x does the curve land back on the ground?" Sometimes there are two landing spots, sometimes one, sometimes none — that's what Idea 04 explains.

Idea 04

The discriminant: how many roots?

Before solving anything, you can peek ahead and know how many roots to expect, using one small formula:

D = b² − 4ac

  • D > 0 → two different real roots (curve crosses the ground twice)
  • D = 0 → one repeated root (curve just touches the ground once)
  • D < 0 → no real roots (curve floats above or below, never touching)
In simple words

The discriminant is like a quick weather check before a trip — it tells you what to expect before you actually start solving.

Idea 05

Where this shows up in real life

That U‑shaped curve isn't just a classroom drawing — it's the actual path of a ball thrown into the air, water arcing from a fountain, the cables of a suspension bridge, and satellite dishes.

  • Throw a ball, and its height over time follows a quadratic equation.
  • Solving it tells you exactly when the ball lands, or how high it goes.
  • Shops and businesses even use similar curves to find the price that gives maximum profit.
In simple words

Anywhere something rises and then falls smoothly — a ball, a fountain, a rocket's path — a quadratic equation is quietly describing it.
